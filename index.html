Economics

Supply and demand

The two forces that set prices: how much of something is available (supply) and how much people want it (demand).

When demand for flights rises in summer, ticket prices go up.

Market

Any place or system where buyers and sellers trade something — goods, services, shares or even jobs.

The smartphone market is dominated by a handful of large companies.

Inflation

A general rise in prices over time, which means your money buys less than before.

With inflation at 4%, groceries cost noticeably more than last year.

GDP (gross domestic product)

The total value of everything a country produces in a year — the standard measure of the size of an economy.

Germany's GDP grew by 1.2% last year.

Recession

A period when the economy shrinks instead of growing, usually with rising unemployment.

Many businesses cut spending during the recession.

Interest rate

The price of borrowing money, shown as a percentage. Higher rates make loans more expensive and saving more attractive.

The central bank raised interest rates to slow inflation.

Central bank

The national institution that manages a country's money supply and sets interest rates, like the European Central Bank.

The central bank cut rates to encourage borrowing.

Monetary policy

What a central bank does to influence the economy, mainly by changing interest rates.

Tight monetary policy helped bring inflation down.

Fiscal policy

What a government does with taxes and public spending to influence the economy.

The government used fiscal policy — tax cuts and new spending — to boost growth.

Opportunity cost

What you give up when you choose one option over another — the value of the next-best alternative.

The opportunity cost of studying full-time is the salary you could have earned working.

Scarcity

The basic problem that resources are limited but wants are not — the starting point of economics.

Because of scarcity, every society must decide how to allocate its resources.

Incentive

Anything that motivates people to act in a certain way, such as a reward or a penalty.

Lower taxes on electric cars are an incentive to buy them.

Unemployment rate

The percentage of people who want a job but cannot find one.

The unemployment rate fell to 5% as the economy recovered.

Exchange rate

The price of one currency in terms of another.

A strong euro exchange rate makes holidays in Australia cheaper for Germans.

Tariff

A tax a country charges on imported goods, usually to protect local producers.

The new tariff on imported steel raised prices for car makers.

Import / export

To import is to buy goods or services from another country; to export is to sell them abroad. Also used as nouns.

Germany exports cars worldwide and imports most of its energy.

Trade surplus / trade deficit

A surplus means a country exports more than it imports; a deficit means the opposite.

Germany has run a trade surplus for decades.

Free trade

Trade between countries without barriers like tariffs or quotas. A free trade agreement (FTA) is a treaty that removes such barriers.

The free trade agreement removed tariffs on most goods between the two countries.

Protectionism

Government policies that shield domestic industries from foreign competition, for example through tariffs or quotas — the opposite of free trade.

Rising protectionism has made global trade more expensive.

Quota

A government limit on how much of a product may be imported.

The quota allows only 50,000 tonnes of sugar imports per year.

Subsidy

Money a government pays to support an industry or lower the price of a product.

Farmers receive subsidies to keep food prices stable.

Sanctions

Penalties, usually trade or financial restrictions, that countries impose on another country for political reasons.

The sanctions cut the country off from international banking.

Comparative advantage

The idea that countries gain by specialising in what they produce relatively best and trading for the rest — the core argument for international trade.

Comparative advantage explains why Switzerland focuses on pharmaceuticals rather than growing bananas.

Multinational corporation (MNC)

A company that operates in many countries at once, like Unilever or Toyota.

Multinational corporations often adapt their products to local markets.

Foreign direct investment (FDI)

When a company invests directly in business operations in another country, such as building a factory abroad.

The region attracted billions in foreign direct investment from tech firms.

Emerging market

A country whose economy is growing quickly and becoming more industrialised, such as India, Brazil or Vietnam.

Many companies see emerging markets as their biggest growth opportunity.

Outsourcing / offshoring

Outsourcing means paying another company to do a task for you; offshoring means moving work to another country. They often overlap.

The airline outsourced its call centre, offshoring the work to the Philippines.

Appreciation / depreciation (of a currency)

A currency appreciates when it becomes worth more against other currencies, and depreciates when it becomes worth less.

When the euro appreciates, German exports become more expensive for American buyers.

Business cycle

The natural rise and fall of economic activity over time — expansion (boom), then slowdown, recession and recovery.

Economists debate where we are in the current business cycle.

GDP per capita

A country's GDP divided by its population — a rough measure of average income and living standards, better for comparing countries than total GDP.

Luxembourg has a small economy but one of the highest GDPs per capita in the world.

Purchasing power

How much your money can actually buy. Inflation reduces purchasing power even if your salary stays the same.

Wages rose 3%, but with 5% inflation, purchasing power actually fell.

WTO, IMF and World Bank

The three big international economic institutions: the WTO sets rules for global trade, the IMF helps countries in financial crisis, and the World Bank funds development projects.

The country turned to the IMF for an emergency loan during the crisis.

Globalisation

The growing connection of economies worldwide through trade, investment and technology.

Globalisation lets a start-up in Berlin sell to customers in Sydney overnight.

Competition

Rivalry between businesses for customers, which tends to push prices down and quality up.

Fierce competition among airlines keeps fares low.

Monopoly

A market with only one seller, giving that company strong power over prices.

The railway operator held a monopoly on the route for years.

Economies of scale

Cost savings that come from producing in larger quantities — the more you make, the cheaper each unit becomes.

Thanks to economies of scale, the factory cut its cost per unit by a third.

Elasticity

How strongly demand or supply reacts when the price changes. If a small price rise makes many people stop buying, demand is "elastic".

Demand for luxury goods is elastic — people simply stop buying when prices rise.

Consumer

A person who buys and uses goods or services — often the focus of business decisions.

Consumers are increasingly choosing sustainable products.

Accounting & finance

Revenue

All the money a business earns from selling its products or services, before any costs are subtracted. Also called "turnover" or "sales".

The company's revenue reached €2 million in its second year.

Costs / expenses

The money a business spends to operate — wages, rent, materials and so on.

Rising energy costs squeezed the restaurant's profits.

Profit

What is left of revenue after all costs are paid. A loss is the opposite.

The shop made a profit of €50,000 last year.

Profit margin

Profit as a percentage of revenue — it shows how much of each euro of sales the business actually keeps.

Supermarkets survive on thin profit margins of just a few per cent.

Gross / net

Gross means before deductions, net means after. Gross profit is revenue minus production costs; net profit is what remains after all costs and tax. Same logic as German brutto/netto.

Her gross salary is €4,000, but the net amount after tax is much lower.

Fixed costs / variable costs

Fixed costs stay the same regardless of how much you produce (like rent); variable costs rise with production (like materials).

The café's rent is a fixed cost, while coffee beans are a variable cost.

Overheads

The ongoing running costs of a business that aren't tied to a specific product — rent, electricity, administration.

Working from home helped the agency cut its overheads.

Asset

Anything valuable a business owns, such as cash, buildings, machines or brands.

The company's biggest asset is its city-centre office building.

Liability

Anything a business owes to others, such as loans or unpaid bills.

The firm reduced its liabilities by paying off its bank loan early.

Equity

The owners' share of a business — what would be left if all assets were sold and all debts paid. Also used for shares in a company.

The founders kept 60% of the equity after the investment round.

Balance sheet

A financial statement showing what a company owns (assets) and owes (liabilities) at one moment in time.

Investors checked the balance sheet before buying shares.

Income statement (profit and loss statement)

A report showing a company's revenue, costs and profit over a period, usually a year. Often shortened to "P&L".

The income statement showed a small loss in the first quarter.

Cash flow

The money actually moving in and out of a business. A company can be profitable on paper but still fail if cash runs out.

Late-paying customers caused a serious cash flow problem.

Budget

A plan for how much money will be earned and spent over a period.

The marketing team went over budget in December.

Investment

Putting money into something — a business, shares, property — hoping it will grow in value or produce income.

Her investment in the start-up tripled in value.

Share (stock)

A small ownership piece of a company. Buying shares makes you a part-owner. "Share" is the usual British term; "stock" is more American.

He bought shares in the company when it listed on the stock exchange.

Shareholder

A person or institution that owns shares in a company.

Shareholders voted to approve the merger at the annual meeting.

Dividend

A portion of profit that a company pays out to its shareholders.

The bank paid a dividend of €1.20 per share.

Debt

Money that is borrowed and must be paid back, usually with interest.

The company took on debt to build its new factory.

Interest

The extra money paid for borrowing, or earned for lending or saving.

The loan charges 6% interest per year.

Return on investment (ROI)

How much profit an investment produces compared with what it cost, usually as a percentage.

The advertising campaign delivered an ROI of 150%.

Break-even point

The point at which revenue exactly covers costs — no profit, no loss.

The café reached its break-even point after eight months.

Depreciation

The gradual loss in value of an asset over time, such as machinery wearing out — recorded as a cost in the accounts.

The delivery vans lose about 20% of their value each year through depreciation.

Bookkeeping / accounting

Bookkeeping is recording every financial transaction; accounting is the broader work of organising, reporting and interpreting those numbers.

She does the bookkeeping herself but hires an accountant for the year-end reports.

Invoice

A document requesting payment for goods or services delivered — the English word for Rechnung in a business context.

Please send the invoice by the end of the month so we can process payment.

Accounts receivable / accounts payable

Receivables are money customers still owe you; payables are money you still owe suppliers.

The firm's accounts receivable grew because customers were paying late.

Liquidity

How easily a company can pay its short-term bills — or how quickly an asset can be turned into cash.

The company sold some equipment to improve its liquidity.

Insolvency / bankruptcy

Insolvency means being unable to pay your debts; bankruptcy is the formal legal process that can follow.

The chain filed for bankruptcy after two years of losses.

Capital

The money and assets a business uses to operate and grow. "Raising capital" means getting funding from investors or lenders.

The start-up raised capital from three investors to fund its expansion.

Stock exchange / stock market

The marketplace where shares of public companies are bought and sold, like the Frankfurt Stock Exchange or the ASX in Sydney.

The company listed on the stock exchange to raise money for growth.

Bond

A loan to a government or company that you can buy as an investment — it pays regular interest and returns your money at the end. Generally safer but less profitable than shares.

Government bonds are popular with cautious investors.

Portfolio

The full collection of investments a person or fund holds.

Her portfolio contains shares, bonds and a little gold.

Diversification

Spreading investments across different assets so one failure doesn't ruin you — the financial version of not putting all your eggs in one basket.

Diversification protected her savings when tech shares crashed.

Risk and return

The basic trade-off in finance: investments that might earn more usually carry a higher chance of loss.

Start-up shares offer high potential returns but come with high risk.

Compound interest

Interest earned on both your original money and the interest already added — which makes savings (and debts) grow faster over time.

Thanks to compound interest, money invested at 20 is worth far more at 60.

IPO (initial public offering)

The first time a company sells its shares to the public on a stock exchange — "going public".

The tech firm's IPO valued it at over a billion euros.

Venture capital (VC)

Investment money given to young, high-risk companies in exchange for a share of ownership.

The start-up secured venture capital to hire its first twenty employees.

Tax / VAT

Tax is money paid to the state. VAT (value added tax) is the tax added to most purchases — the equivalent of the German Mehrwertsteuer. Note: Australia calls it GST.

Prices in the shop include 19% VAT.

Audit

An official, independent check of a company's financial records to make sure they are accurate.

The annual audit found no irregularities in the accounts.

Recurring (recurring revenue / recurring payment)

Happening again and again on a regular schedule. Recurring revenue is income that repeats automatically, like subscriptions — investors love it because it's predictable.

The software firm's subscriptions generate recurring revenue every month.

Forecast

An estimate of future results, such as next year's sales, based on current information.

The sales forecast for next quarter looks optimistic.

Statistics & data

Data

Facts and figures collected for analysis. Note: in everyday English "data" is usually treated as singular ("the data shows…").

The data shows that sales peak on Saturdays.

Average (mean)

The typical value of a set of numbers: add them all up and divide by how many there are.

The average customer spends €35 per visit.

Median

The middle value when numbers are sorted from smallest to largest — often more honest than the average when a few extreme values distort the picture.

The median salary gives a better picture than the mean, because a few very high earners pull the mean up.

Mode

The value that appears most often in a set of data — the German Modus.

The mode of shoe sizes sold was 42 — the most common size by far.

Percentage / percentage point

A share out of 100. A "percentage point" is the difference between two percentages — going from 4% to 6% is a rise of two percentage points.

Market share rose from 10% to 12% — an increase of two percentage points.

Growth rate

How fast something increases over time, usually as a percentage per year.

The company's growth rate slowed from 15% to 8% a year.

Population

In statistics, the entire group you want to study — all customers, all students, all voters. The German Grundgesamtheit.

The population for the study was all first-year students in Australia.

Sample

A smaller group selected from a larger population to study, because surveying everyone is impractical.

The survey used a sample of 1,000 customers.

Random sample

A sample where every member of the population has an equal chance of being chosen — the fairest way to get results that represent the whole group.

They drew a random sample instead of just surveying people outside one shop.

Bias

Anything that systematically distorts results in one direction, such as surveying only your happiest customers.

The survey suffered from bias because only satisfied customers bothered to respond.

Qualitative / quantitative data

Quantitative data is numbers (sales, age, prices); qualitative data is descriptions (opinions, interview answers, categories).

The study combined quantitative sales data with qualitative customer interviews.

Survey

A set of questions asked to many people to gather information about opinions or behaviour.

A recent survey found that 70% of students prefer online lectures.

Variable

Anything that can take different values and be measured, such as price, age or sales.

Price and demand are the two key variables in the model.

Correlation

When two things tend to move together. Important: correlation does not prove that one causes the other.

There is a strong correlation between advertising spend and sales.

Trend

The general direction in which something is moving over time — upwards, downwards or flat.

Online sales show a clear upward trend.

Distribution

How values are spread out across a range — for example, whether most salaries cluster in the middle or at the extremes.

Exam marks followed a normal distribution, with most students near the middle.

Standard deviation

A measure of how spread out numbers are around the average. Small means values are close together; large means they vary a lot.

The two funds have the same average return, but one has a much higher standard deviation — it is riskier.

Variance

Another measure of spread — the standard deviation squared. You'll meet both in formulas; they tell the same story.

Higher variance in monthly sales makes planning harder.

Quartile / percentile

Ways of ranking values by position: quartiles split sorted data into four equal parts; percentiles into a hundred. Being in the 90th percentile means 90% scored below you.

Her entrance exam score was in the top quartile.

Probability

The chance that something will happen, from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain), often written as a percentage.

There is a 30% probability the project will run over budget.

Outlier

A value far outside the normal range of the data, which can distort averages.

One enormous order was an outlier that made the monthly figures look better than they were.

Benchmark

A standard point of comparison used to judge performance.

The team compared its costs against an industry benchmark.

Causation

When one thing directly causes another. The classic warning in every statistics course: correlation alone does not prove causation.

Ice cream sales and drownings are correlated, but the causation is hot weather driving both.

Hypothesis

A precise claim you test with data, such as "the new design increases sales". Statistics courses spend a lot of time on hypothesis testing.

The team tested the hypothesis that free shipping increases order size.

Statistical significance (p-value)

A result is statistically significant when it's unlikely to have happened by pure chance. The p-value measures this — smaller means stronger evidence, with 0.05 the usual threshold.

The increase in sales was statistically significant, with a p-value below 0.05.

Confidence interval

A range that likely contains the true value, since a sample never gives you the exact answer — usually stated with 95% confidence.

Average spend was €42, with a 95% confidence interval of €39 to €45.

Regression

A method for estimating how one variable affects another, such as how much sales rise per euro of advertising. One of the most-used tools in business analysis.

The regression showed that each €1,000 of advertising added roughly €4,500 in sales.

Bar chart, pie chart, histogram, scatter plot

The everyday chart vocabulary: bar charts compare categories, pie charts show shares of a whole, histograms show distributions, and scatter plots show the relationship between two variables — plotted on the x-axis (horizontal) and y-axis (vertical).

She used a scatter plot to show the relationship between price and demand.

Spreadsheet

Software like Excel or Google Sheets that organises data in rows and columns — the standard tool for data work in any business degree.

The assignment requires you to analyse the dataset in a spreadsheet.

Dataset

An organised collection of data, often in a table or spreadsheet, ready for analysis.

We analysed a dataset of 10,000 customer transactions.

General business & management

Strategy

A company's long-term plan for reaching its goals — where to compete and how to win.

Their strategy is to focus on premium customers rather than compete on price.

Business model

How a company makes money: what it sells, to whom, and how it earns more than it spends.

Netflix changed its business model from DVD rental to streaming subscriptions.

Value proposition / USP

The value proposition is why customers should choose you; the USP (unique selling point) is the one thing that sets you apart from competitors.

Free next-day delivery is the shop's USP.

Mission and vision

The mission says what a company does and why; the vision describes what it wants to become. Standard vocabulary in every strategy course.

The company's mission is affordable design; its vision is to be Europe's leading furniture brand.

Stakeholder

Anyone affected by a business or with an interest in it: employees, customers, investors, suppliers, the local community.

The company consulted all stakeholders before closing the plant.

CEO, CFO, COO (the C-suite)

The top executives: the CEO (chief executive officer) leads the company, the CFO (chief financial officer) manages the money, the COO (chief operating officer) runs daily operations. Together they're called the C-suite.

The CEO announced the new strategy, and the CFO explained how it would be funded.

Board of directors

The group elected by shareholders to oversee company management and take major decisions. Note: the German split between Vorstand and Aufsichtsrat doesn't exist in most English-speaking countries — one board does both jobs.

The board of directors approved the acquisition.

Headquarters (HQ) / subsidiary

Headquarters is a company's main office; a subsidiary is a company owned and controlled by another (parent) company.

The group's headquarters are in Munich, with subsidiaries in twelve countries.

Department / division

The units a company is organised into — marketing department, finance department. A division is usually bigger, often running its own product line.

She works in the marketing department of the consumer goods division.

Operations

The day-to-day work of actually producing and delivering what a company sells. "Ops" for short.

Smooth operations are what keep delivery times short.

Organisational structure / hierarchy

How roles and reporting lines are arranged in a company. A "flat" hierarchy has few management layers; a "steep" one has many.

The start-up's flat hierarchy means everyone reports directly to the founders.

Corporate culture

The shared values and unwritten rules of how people work together in a company.

The firm's corporate culture encourages people to challenge their managers openly.

Customer vs client

Both buy from a business. "Customer" is general; "client" usually means someone receiving professional or ongoing services, like at a law firm.

The consultancy meets each client monthly, while the shop serves hundreds of customers a day.

Market share

The percentage of a market's total sales that one company captures.

The brand grew its market share from 8% to 12% in two years.

Competitor

Another business trying to sell to the same customers as you.

Their main competitor launched a cheaper version last month.

Brand

The identity of a product or company — its name, image and the feelings people associate with it.

A strong brand lets the company charge higher prices.

Marketing

Everything a business does to understand customers and persuade them to buy — research, advertising, pricing, promotion.

The marketing campaign targeted students on social media.

Sales

The function of actually selling to customers — finding them, persuading them and closing deals. Related to marketing but not the same thing.

Marketing brings in the leads; sales turns them into paying customers.

Product launch

The official introduction of a new product to the market.

The product launch was timed for the Christmas shopping season.

Supplier / vendor

A company that provides goods or services to another business. "Vendor" is common in more formal or IT contexts.

The bakery switched suppliers to get better flour at a lower price.

Retail / wholesale

Retail is selling to end consumers in small quantities; wholesale is selling in bulk to other businesses, usually cheaper per unit.

The brand sells wholesale to supermarkets and retail through its own online shop.

Supply chain

The whole journey of a product from raw materials to the final customer, including all the suppliers and transport in between.

The pandemic disrupted global supply chains for electronics.

Logistics

The practical work of moving and storing goods — transport, warehousing, delivery.

Fast, reliable logistics is central to online retail.

Human resources (HR)

The department that handles everything about employees: hiring, pay, training and workplace issues.

HR is running interviews for the graduate programme this week.

Recruitment / onboarding

Recruitment is finding and hiring new employees; onboarding is helping them settle in during their first weeks.

Good onboarding helps new hires become productive within their first month.

Entrepreneur

Someone who starts and builds a business, taking on the risk in hope of reward.

The entrepreneur launched her third company before turning thirty.

Start-up

A young company, often built around a new idea and aiming to grow quickly.

He joined a fintech start-up straight after graduating.

Merger and acquisition (M&A)

A merger is when two companies join to become one; an acquisition is when one company buys another.

The merger created the largest airline in Europe.

KPI (key performance indicator)

A specific number used to measure how well a business or team is performing, such as monthly sales or customer satisfaction.

Customer retention is our most important KPI this year.

Target

A specific goal a person or team is expected to reach.

The sales team hit its quarterly target a week early.

B2B / B2C

B2B (business-to-business) means selling to other companies; B2C (business-to-consumer) means selling to individuals.

The software firm is purely B2B — its customers are other companies.

SWOT analysis

A simple planning tool that lists a business's Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities and Threats.

The team started the project with a SWOT analysis of the brand.

Pitch

A short, persuasive presentation of an idea or business, often to investors. Also used as a verb.

She pitched her business idea to a panel of investors in five minutes.

Negotiation

A discussion aimed at reaching an agreement, where each side may give something up.

After weeks of negotiation, the two firms agreed on a price.

Agenda and minutes

The agenda is the list of topics for a meeting; the minutes are the written record of what was discussed and decided.

Please read the agenda before the meeting — I'll circulate the minutes afterwards.

Feedback

Comments on someone's work or on a product, meant to help improve it.

Her manager gave constructive feedback on the presentation.

Delegate

To give a task or responsibility to someone else, usually someone you manage.

Good managers delegate work instead of doing everything themselves.

Innovation / disruption

Innovation is creating something new and valuable; disruption is when an innovation overturns an existing market, the way streaming disrupted video rental.

Digital cameras disrupted the film industry almost overnight.

Project management (milestone, deliverable)

Planning and steering a project to finish on time and on budget. A milestone is a key checkpoint; a deliverable is a concrete result you must hand over.

The first milestone is the prototype; the final deliverable is the market-ready product.

Efficiency vs effectiveness

A classic management distinction: efficiency is doing things with minimal waste ("doing things right"); effectiveness is achieving the right goals ("doing the right things").

The team was efficient but not effective — they built the wrong product quickly.

Networking

Building professional relationships that may lead to opportunities, advice or jobs.

She met her future employer while networking at a careers fair.

Corporate social responsibility (CSR)

A company's efforts to act ethically and benefit society and the environment, beyond just making profit.

The firm's CSR programme funds local schools and cuts its carbon emissions.

AI & tech

API (application programming interface)

A way for two software systems to talk to each other and exchange data automatically.

The app uses the bank's API to show your balance in real time.

Algorithm

A set of step-by-step instructions a computer follows to solve a problem or make a decision.

The recommendation algorithm decides which videos you see next.

Artificial intelligence (AI)

Software that performs tasks normally requiring human intelligence, such as understanding language or recognising images.

The company uses AI to answer routine customer questions.

Machine learning

A type of AI that improves by learning patterns from data instead of following fixed rules.

The fraud detection system uses machine learning to spot unusual transactions.

Large language model (LLM)

An AI system trained on huge amounts of text to understand and generate language — the technology behind tools like ChatGPT and Claude.

Many firms now use LLMs to draft reports and summarise documents.

Generative AI

AI that creates new content — text, images, code, audio — rather than just analysing existing data.

The agency uses generative AI to draft first versions of ad copy.

Prompt

The instruction or question you give an AI tool. Writing good prompts is becoming a workplace skill of its own.

A more specific prompt gave the AI much better results.

Chatbot

A program that holds conversations with users, often for customer service on websites.

The chatbot resolves most routine questions before a human gets involved.

Model / training data

In AI, the model is the system that makes predictions; training data is the collection of examples it learned from. Poor training data means poor results.

The model performs badly on older customers because they were missing from the training data.

Automation

Using technology to do tasks with little or no human involvement.

Automation of invoicing saved the finance team ten hours a week.

Cloud computing (the cloud)

Using computing power and storage over the internet instead of on your own machines.

The start-up runs everything in the cloud, so it never bought its own servers.

SaaS (software as a service)

Software you use through the internet for a subscription fee instead of buying and installing it — think Zoom or Salesforce.

Most business tools today follow a SaaS model with monthly pricing.

Platform

A digital business that connects two groups, such as buyers and sellers or drivers and riders.

Airbnb is a platform — it owns no rooms but connects hosts with guests.

E-commerce

Buying and selling goods or services online.

E-commerce now accounts for a fifth of all retail sales.

Fintech

Short for "financial technology" — companies using technology to offer financial services, like mobile banking or payment apps.

Fintech start-ups are challenging traditional banks on fees and speed.

Big data

Extremely large sets of data that businesses analyse to find patterns and make decisions.

The retailer uses big data to predict what customers will buy next season.

Data analytics

Examining data to draw useful conclusions for decision-making.

Data analytics showed that most customers abandon their basket at the payment step.

Dashboard

A screen that displays a business's key numbers at a glance, usually with charts that update automatically.

The sales dashboard shows revenue and targets in real time.

Database

An organised digital collection of information that can be searched and updated, such as all customer records.

Every order is stored in the customer database.

Server / hosting

A server is a computer that delivers websites, apps or data to users; hosting is the service of running them for you.

The website crashed because the server couldn't handle the traffic.

Open source

Software whose code is publicly available for anyone to use, inspect and improve — often free, like Linux or Firefox.

The start-up built its product on open-source software to save licensing costs.

Scalability

How well a business or system can grow without costs growing just as fast. Software scales well: serving a million users costs little more than serving a thousand.

Investors loved the app's scalability — growth costs almost nothing.

SEO (search engine optimisation)

Improving a website so it appears higher in Google results and attracts more visitors without paid ads.

Better SEO doubled the shop's traffic within six months.

Internet of Things (IoT)

Everyday objects — machines, vehicles, sensors — connected to the internet so they can send data and be controlled remotely.

IoT sensors alert the factory before a machine breaks down.

Cybersecurity

Protecting computer systems and data from attacks, theft and damage.

After the data breach, the firm doubled its cybersecurity budget.

Phishing

Fraudulent messages that pretend to be from a trusted source to trick people into revealing passwords or payment details.

The fake "IT department" email was a phishing attempt.

Data privacy / GDPR

Data privacy is protecting people's personal information; the GDPR is the EU law governing it — the English name for the DSGVO.

Under GDPR, customers can ask a company to delete their personal data.

Encryption

Scrambling data so only authorised people can read it — the basis of secure messaging and online payments.

The payment data is protected by encryption from your phone to the bank.

Digital transformation

The process of a company rebuilding how it works around digital technology — a very common phrase in business courses and job adverts.

The insurer's digital transformation moved all claims handling online.

Agile

A way of working in short cycles with frequent feedback, instead of planning everything upfront. Originally from software, now used across business.

The team works in an agile way, releasing improvements every two weeks.

MVP (minimum viable product)

The simplest version of a product that works, released early to test whether customers actually want it.

They launched an MVP after three months and improved it based on user feedback.

Beta

An early test version of a product shared with a limited group before the official launch.

The app is still in beta, so some features are missing.

Bug

An error in software that makes it behave incorrectly.

A bug in the checkout page cost the shop a weekend of sales.

UX / UI (user experience / user interface)

UI is what a digital product looks like; UX is how it feels to use — how easy, fast and pleasant it is.

Good UX is why some banking apps feel effortless and others feel frustrating.

Blockchain

A shared digital record of transactions that no single party controls — the technology behind cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin.

Some supply chains use blockchain to track goods from factory to shelf.

University & campus life

Lecture

A large class where a professor presents material and students mostly listen and take notes.

The economics lecture on Monday mornings fills a 300-seat hall.

Seminar

A smaller class built around discussion, where students are expected to participate actively.

Come prepared to the seminar — everyone has to contribute to the discussion.

Tutorial

A small-group session, common in Australia and the UK, where a tutor works through problems and questions from the lectures. Often shortened to "tute" in Australia.

The weekly tutorial is where you actually practise what the lecture covered.

Syllabus

The document describing what a course covers: topics, readings, assessments and deadlines. Read it carefully in week one.

The syllabus says the final exam counts for 50% of the mark.

Course / module / unit

A single subject you study for one semester. Different universities use different words — Sydney says "unit of study", others say "module" or "course".

I'm taking four units this semester, including introductory accounting.

Credits (credit points)

Points you earn for completing each course. You need a set total to graduate.

This unit is worth six credit points.

Semester / term

The blocks the academic year is divided into — usually two semesters, each ending in exams.

Semester one ends with exams in June.

Assignment

Any piece of assessed work you complete outside class, such as an essay, report or problem set.

The first assignment is a 1,500-word report due in week five.

Deadline

The date and time by which work must be submitted. Missing one usually costs marks.

The deadline for the essay is Friday at 5 pm sharp.

Extension

Extra time to submit an assignment, granted if you have a good reason and ask in advance.

She was granted a one-week extension because she was ill.

Office hours

Set times when a professor or tutor is available in their office (or online) for students' questions — free help, use it.

I went to office hours to ask about the exam format.

Plagiarism

Presenting someone else's work or ideas as your own without crediting them. Universities treat it very seriously.

The university uses software to detect plagiarism in every submitted essay.

Citation / reference

The formal note showing where an idea or quote came from. Your reference list at the end of an essay collects them all.

Every claim in your essay needs a citation in the correct format.

GPA (grade point average)

A single number summarising your grades across all courses — the common measure of academic performance.

You need a GPA of at least 5.0 to apply for the exchange programme.

Transcript

The official record of all the courses you have taken and the grades you received.

The internship application asked for an official transcript.

Undergraduate / postgraduate

An undergraduate is studying for a first degree (like a bachelor's); a postgraduate has finished one and is studying further (like a master's).

The library has separate study areas for undergraduates and postgraduates.

Major / minor

Your major is your main subject of specialisation within a degree; a minor is a smaller, secondary one.

She's doing a commerce degree with a major in finance and a minor in marketing.

Faculty

A large academic division of a university, such as the Faculty of Business. In American English it can also mean the teaching staff themselves.

The Business School is part of the Faculty of Economics and Business.

Dean

The senior academic who leads a faculty or school.

The Dean welcomed new students at the opening ceremony.

Lecturer / professor / tutor

A lecturer teaches courses; professor is a senior academic title; a tutor runs small-group tutorials. In Australia and the UK, not every teacher is called "professor".

Our lecturer covers the theory, and the tutor helps us apply it in tutorials.

Enrolment

Officially registering for your degree or for specific courses each semester.

Enrolment for semester two opens in early November.

Orientation (O-Week / freshers' week)

The introduction week before classes start, with campus tours, information sessions and social events. Called "O-Week" in Australia.

Sign up for clubs during O-Week — it's the easiest time to meet people.

Campus

The university's grounds and buildings — lecture halls, libraries, cafés and everything in between.

The campus has three libraries and its own gym.

Student union / student society

The student union is the organisation representing students; societies (or clubs) are student groups for hobbies, sports and career interests.

She joined the business society through the student union.

Exam / finals / mid-terms

Formal tests. "Mid-terms" happen partway through the semester; "finals" are the big exams at the end.

The library is packed during finals.

Group work / group project

An assignment completed by a team of students together — very common in business degrees.

Half our marketing grade comes from a group project and presentation.

Presentation

Speaking in front of the class to explain your work, usually with slides. A core skill in business programmes.

Each group gives a ten-minute presentation in the final week.

Internship / placement

A temporary job, often during holidays, where you gain real work experience. "Placement" is the more British term when it is part of the degree.

He did a summer internship at a consulting firm after first year.

Exchange / study abroad

Spending a semester or year studying at a partner university in another country.

She spent her exchange semester at a university in Singapore.

Scholarship

Money awarded to a student to help pay for their studies, usually based on merit or need — it does not have to be repaid.

He won a scholarship that covers half his tuition fees.

Dissertation / thesis

A long piece of independent research written towards the end of a degree. Less common in bachelor's programmes, but worth knowing.

Final-year students spend months on their dissertation.

All terms are standard first-year business and university vocabulary — none are obscure or invented.
